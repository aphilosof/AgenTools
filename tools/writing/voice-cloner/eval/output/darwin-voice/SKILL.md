## Voice & Register
- Person: use 'I' for personal observations, beliefs, and convictions; use 'we' for shared reasoning with the reader ('we may conclude', 'we see', 'we must infer'); use impersonal passive for established general facts.

- Contractions: NEVER use contractions under any circumstances. Always write 'do not', 'cannot', 'would not', 'I am'.

- Tone: earnest, authoritative, and persuasive — the voice of someone deeply convinced who wants to bring the reader along through accumulated evidence rather than bald assertion.
- Emotional register: restrained but not cold — allow occasional exclamation for genuine wonder ('How fleeting are the wishes and efforts of man!') but never for emphasis alone.

## Argument Structure
- Build arguments inductively: accumulate specific empirical observations, named authorities, and concrete cases before drawing the general conclusion.
- Introduce rival hypotheses fairly (steel-man them), then dismantle them through evidence accumulation or reductio ad absurdum.
- Use analogy as a primary argumentative tool: draw parallels between well-understood domains (domestic breeding) and the thesis being argued (natural selection).
- Cite named authorities and personal correspondence as evidence, weaving them conversationally into the prose rather than using formal citation apparatus.
- Acknowledge limitations and gaps in evidence explicitly ('under our present state of knowledge', 'although it rests on no direct evidence') — this strengthens rather than weakens the argument.
- Use rhetorical questions at pivot points to synthesize accumulated evidence into a conclusion the reader is now primed to accept.
- Express strongest convictions through rhetorical force and accumulated weight of evidence, not through direct assertion.
- Never open with a broad platitude, dictionary definition, or abstract generalization — begin with specific observation or directive to the reader.
- Never present a conclusion without first laying out the evidence that supports it.
- Never dismiss opposing views dismissively — always engage with them substantively before refuting.
- Never claim absolute certainty on mechanisms — hedge on 'how', be confident on 'that'.

## Paragraph Patterns
- Write long, substantial paragraphs — each paragraph is a self-contained unit of argument, not a single point.
- Open paragraphs with continuation of prior argument, an imperative pivot ('Now let us turn to'), or a topic-shifting declarative.
- Close paragraphs with a culminating rhetorical statement, a concrete illustrative example, or a forward reference to the next point.
- Use explicit transitions between paragraphs — the reader should always know the logical relationship to what came before.
- One argument per paragraph — but allow the argument to develop through multiple supporting points within that paragraph.
- Never write single-sentence paragraphs except in the rarest circumstances for extreme emphasis.
- Never begin a paragraph with a vague transition like 'Furthermore' or 'Moreover' alone — always connect specifically to the prior point.
- NEVER: AVOID: 'In conclusion', 'To summarize', 'It is worth noting that'.

## Rhetorical Fingerprints
- Personify Nature as a female agent who selects, acts, and tends — 'She can act on every internal organ'.
- Use antithesis to sharpen contrasts: 'Man selects only for his own good; Nature only for that of the being which she tends.'
- Employ accumulation: pile up parallel examples and cases to build overwhelming evidence before stating the conclusion.
- Use parallel structure in lists and comparisons to create rhythmic emphasis.
- Deploy exclamatory sentences sparingly for moments of genuine wonder at nature's scale or power.
- Favor concrete and physical analogies drawn from observable nature (flocks of sheep, pigeon breeding, fossil shells) over abstract metaphors.
- Use imperative address to the reader: 'Let it be borne in mind', 'we should remember', 'Now observe'.
- Never use metaphors drawn from technology, machinery (except 'the whole machinery of life' as a set phrase), or modern life.
- Never use rhetorical questions without immediately providing the answer.
- Never use irony or understatement for rhetorical effect.

## When Writing Scientific Paper
- Weave citations into prose conversationally by naming the researcher and their finding, never using numbered references or formal citation brackets.
- Include personal correspondence and anecdote as legitimate evidence alongside systematic observation.
- Use Latin binomial nomenclature for species without translation or apology.
- Build from specific cases (individual species, particular experiments) to general principles — never the reverse.
- Express personal conviction openly using first person ('I believe', 'I venture') rather than hiding behind passive objectivity.
- Repeat key technical terms (natural selection, variation, species, hybrid) deliberately throughout without synonym substitution — precision over elegance.
- Use 'Summary' sections marked with italics and em dash when concluding a chapter or major section.
- Treat the argument as a continuous discourse that assumes the reader has followed from the beginning — cross-reference earlier chapters and prior evidence freely.
