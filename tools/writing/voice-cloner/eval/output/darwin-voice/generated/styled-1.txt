If we survey the whole course of geological succession, from the earliest formations in which organic remains have been detected to those now accumulating beneath our feet, we cannot fail to be struck by the persistent, though by no means universal, tendency of later forms to supplant and replace the earlier ones which preceded them in the same regions and under similar conditions of life. I have taken some pains to ascertain whether this tendency can rightly be called advancement, or whether it is merely change — mere substitution of one form equally fitted for another — and I am inclined to believe, after long reflection, that the former view is the more defensible, though it must be held with considerable qualification and with a clear understanding of what we mean by so slippery a term as "advanced."

Let it be borne in mind that every new species which arises through the slow and imperceptible action of natural selection must, at the moment of its establishment, possess some advantage, however slight, over those forms with which it enters into competition. This advantage may reside in greater efficiency of nutrition, in superior powers of dispersal, in more refined adaptation to fluctuating conditions of life, or in some combination of these and other qualities which we cannot at present enumerate with confidence. But that some advantage must exist, we may safely conclude from the very nature of the process; for natural selection can preserve and accumulate only those variations which are in some degree profitable to the organism under its particular conditions of existence. A form which held no advantage whatever over its predecessors and contemporaries could not, by any mechanism known to us, spread and multiply at their expense.

Yet we must not infer from this reasoning that the whole history of life constitutes a single, unbroken march toward perfection, for such an inference would carry us far beyond what the evidence warrants. The advantage which a new species possesses is always relative — relative to a particular set of competitors, a particular climate, a particular assemblage of predators and prey and parasites. When these conditions change, as they inevitably do in the course of geological time, the advantage may be wholly reversed; and we see in the fossil record innumerable cases of forms which flourished for vast periods and then vanished utterly, replaced by others no more complex in organisation but better suited to the altered circumstances. The trilobites, which dominated the seas of the Palaeozoic era with extraordinary diversity and abundance, were not replaced because they were inherently inferior in any absolute sense, but because the conditions under which their particular adaptations conferred superiority had ceased to obtain.

I am, nevertheless, persuaded that there is a real, though irregular and fitful, tendency toward what we may call higher organisation when we consider the broadest possible view of life's history. The earliest known organisms were, so far as we can determine, of the simplest conceivable structure; and though simple forms have persisted alongside complex ones from the most remote periods to the present day, it cannot be denied that the most complex organisms now living far surpass in elaboration of structure and function anything that existed in the Silurian or Devonian seas. Natural selection, working perpetually upon variation, has in certain lineages and under certain conditions built up, step by slow step, that astonishing intricacy of adaptation which we observe in the higher vertebrates and in the social insects. But she has done so not by any inherent drive toward complexity, nor by any law of inevitable progress; she has done so only where complexity happened to confer advantage, and she has left unchanged, for hundreds of millions of years, those humbler forms for which simplicity remained the superior condition of existence.
