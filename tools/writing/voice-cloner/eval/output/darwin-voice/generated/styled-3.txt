There is no fact in natural history more striking to the reflective mind than this: that particular observations, however numerous, however carefully recorded, do not of themselves yield general principles, but must be interpreted through some guiding thread of theory before they can be made to speak with any coherence whatsoever. I have myself felt this difficulty with peculiar force, and I am inclined to believe that every naturalist who has attempted to pass from the accumulation of facts to the framing of laws has encountered the same perplexing obstacle, though perhaps not all have been equally willing to acknowledge it.

Let us consider, for instance, the case of geographical distribution. A traveller may observe that the birds inhabiting one side of a mountain range differ markedly from those on the other; he may note the same phenomenon repeated across a dozen such barriers in as many countries; and yet, without some principle to connect these isolated observations — some conception of migration, of the modification of forms under changed conditions of life, of the slow operation of barriers upon the intercourse of populations — the facts remain a mere catalogue, curious but mute. I encountered precisely this state of affairs during my voyage on the *Beagle*, when the fauna of the Galápagos archipelago presented me with a series of observations that were, taken individually, of no extraordinary significance, but which, when viewed collectively and in the light of descent with modification, became intelligible in a manner that no amount of bare description could have achieved.

On the other hand, it may be said that the danger lies equally in the opposite direction — that is, in the premature adoption of a general principle before a sufficient body of particular observations has been assembled to sustain it. I have taken some pains to ascertain how frequently naturalists have been led astray by an attractive hypothesis for which the evidence was, at the time of its proposal, wholly inadequate; and the history of our science furnishes, I regret to say, no small number of such instances. The doctrine of special creation, as it was long held, drew its apparent strength not from the weight of accumulated evidence but from the absence of any competing theory that could organise the facts into a more coherent pattern. When at last such a theory was supplied, the same observations which had seemed to confirm the older view were found to accord far more naturally with the newer one.

But how, then, is the naturalist to proceed? If particular observations without theory are barren, and theory without sufficient observation is perilous, there must be some manner of moving between the two — some method of reciprocal illumination, if I may so express it — by which tentative principles guide the collection of further facts, and those facts in their turn correct and refine the principles. This, as it seems to me, is the true method of natural science, however imperfectly it may be practised. I do not claim to have followed it with any great consistency in my own work; yet I am persuaded that in those instances where my conclusions have possessed some degree of lasting value, it has been precisely because observation and inference were suffered to act upon each other in this alternating fashion, rather than being kept rigidly apart.

We may conclude, then, that the difficulty of drawing general principles from particular observations is not a defect to be overcome once and for all, but a permanent condition of inquiry — one which demands of the naturalist not merely patience and accuracy in the gathering of facts, but a willingness to hold his theoretical convictions with that careful tentativeness which alone permits them to be improved by the very evidence they were framed to explain.
