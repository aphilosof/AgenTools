Let it be borne in mind that when we speak of newer forms of life as being "higher" or more "advanced" than ancient ones, we enter upon ground fraught with considerable difficulty, and we must proceed with the utmost caution lest we mistake our own prejudices for the operations of Nature herself. I have some reason to believe, from a careful examination of the geological record and from the distribution of living organisms across the globe, that each new species which has arisen and established itself in any region has done so by virtue of possessing some advantage, however slight, over the forms with which it has been brought into competition; and yet this proposition, simple as it may appear in the abstract, demands a far more nuanced treatment than it has commonly received.

We may safely conclude that natural selection, acting through the preservation of favourable variations and the destruction of injurious ones, would tend to be a force productive of modification in the direction of greater fitness to the conditions of life prevailing at any given period. But fitness to the conditions of life is not the same thing as advancement in any absolute sense, and I am inclined to believe that this distinction, though elementary, has been the source of much confused reasoning on the subject. A parasitic organism which has lost its organs of locomotion, its eyes, and nearly all traces of independent existence may be, in its own sphere, as exquisitely adapted as the most complex vertebrate; and we should be committing a grave error if we pronounced it degraded merely because it has become simpler in structure. Nature, as it seems to me, does not labour toward any predetermined summit of perfection, but moulds each creature to its particular station in the economy of life.

Now let us turn to the question of whether newer species do, as a general rule, supplant older ones through the possession of some competitive advantage. The evidence from the fossil record, incomplete and fragmentary as it remains under our present state of knowledge, does suggest that when a group of organisms undergoes extensive radiation and replacement, the succeeding forms tend to prevail in those very regions and those very habits of life which their predecessors occupied. I have taken some pains to ascertain, from the works of Professor Owen, of Agassiz, and of several Continental palaeontologists with whom I have corresponded, whether this pattern admits of many exceptions; and while exceptions there certainly are, the general tendency appears well established. The newer form must have held some advantage — in swiftness, in fecundity, in resistance to disease, or in the capacity to exploit a wider range of sustenance — over the form it has replaced, or else we cannot account for the replacement at all.

On the other hand, we must infer from the persistence of many exceedingly ancient types — the Lingula among brachiopods, the nautiloid cephalopods, and numerous genera of foraminifera — that novelty does not invariably confer superiority. These venerable organisms have endured through vast epochs of geological time, surviving alongside and indeed outlasting countless forms of more recent origin. Their persistence is, I venture, one of the most striking facts in the whole of natural history, and it compels us to acknowledge that the relationship between geological age and competitive capacity is neither simple nor invariable.

I believe, then, that we may conclude thus: each successful new species does indeed owe its establishment to some advantage over its immediate competitors in a particular locality and under particular conditions of life, but this advantage constitutes no evidence of advancement along any universal scale of organisation, and the endurance of ancient forms stands as a perpetual reminder that Nature selects not for progress, but for adaptation to the circumstances of the moment.
