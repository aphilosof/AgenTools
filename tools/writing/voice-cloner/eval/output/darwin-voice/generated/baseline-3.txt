Every morning I watch the same crow land on the telephone wire outside my kitchen window. It arrives at roughly seven fifteen, caws three times, and flies north. For weeks I noted this pattern, and I began to feel confident in a principle: crows are creatures of strict routine. Then one Tuesday the crow didn't come. It didn't come Wednesday either. By Thursday I had to admit that my general principle was built on a foundation of maybe twenty data points, all drawn from a single bird on a single wire in a single season.

This is the problem I keep running into. I observe something specific, something concrete and undeniable in itself, and I feel the pull toward abstraction. The particular wants to become universal. A friend betrays a confidence, and suddenly I'm constructing theories about human nature. A student fails despite studying hard, and I start questioning whether effort really matters. The leap from *this happened* to *this is how things work* feels so natural that I rarely notice I'm making it.

I think about this when I cook. For years I believed that searing meat "locks in the juices"—a principle I derived from watching my mother cook steaks on a cast-iron skillet. The meat sizzled, a crust formed, and the result was juicy. Cause and effect seemed obvious. But the principle is wrong. Food scientists have weighed seared and unseared pieces of meat and found no meaningful difference in moisture loss. What I observed was real. What I concluded was false. The crust tasted good for its own reasons, and the juiciness had more to do with temperature and timing. I had confused a correlation visible in my kitchen for a causal law operating everywhere.

The harder version of this problem is when the particular observation is emotionally charged. I once invested months in a project that failed despite what I considered rigorous planning. The temptation was enormous to extract a lesson—maybe that planning is overrated, maybe that the market is irrational, maybe that I should trust my gut instead of spreadsheets. But each of those "lessons" would have been a general principle resting on one painful experience. The failure could have been caused by a dozen factors I didn't fully understand. Drawing a confident rule from it would have been like diagnosing an engine problem by listening to a single knock.

What I've come to suspect is that the difficulty isn't just intellectual—it's temperamental. I want principles. I want the world to be the kind of place where enough careful watching yields reliable rules. And sometimes it does. Gravity works every time I drop a glass. But most of the things I actually care about—how people behave, what makes a creative project succeed, when to speak and when to stay quiet—operate in systems so tangled that any principle I extract is, at best, a rough heuristic with an invisible list of exceptions.

So I've started holding my generalizations more loosely. I still make them, because I don't know how to think without them. But I try to remember the crow. I try to remember that what I saw was a bird on a wire twenty mornings in a row, not a law of ornithology. The observation was solid. The principle was a guess dressed up as knowledge. Keeping that distinction alive is harder than it sounds, and I fail at it regularly. Which, I suppose, I should be careful about turning into a general principle of its own.
