There's a romantic story founders love to tell themselves. Build something incredible, put it out into the world, and watch it spread like wildfire. Users will come. The product will speak for itself. Growth will be organic, natural, inevitable.

It's a beautiful story. It's also mostly bullshit.

The products we think of as overnight successes almost never were. Behind every "viral" launch was a founder who spent months begging people to try the thing. Who personally onboarded the first hundred users. Who wrote individual emails, made phone calls, showed up at events, and did things that absolutely do not scale — because that's what it takes to get the flywheel moving when it has zero momentum.

Airbnb's founders went door to door in New York, photographing apartments themselves. Stripe's founders would literally grab people's laptops and install the integration for them — they called it "Collison installation." DoorDash founders delivered the food themselves. These weren't growth hacks. They were acts of sheer will from people who refused to sit around waiting for the market to come to them.

And here's what most people miss: this isn't just a phase you push through to get to the "real" growth. This IS the real growth. The pushing, the grinding, the manual and ugly and deeply personal work of convincing one human being at a time that your thing is worth their attention — that's the foundation everything else gets built on.

Because traction doesn't just happen to you. Traction is something you go out and create.

I think the "build it and they will come" myth persists because it flatters us. It tells us that quality alone is enough. That if we're smart enough, creative enough, technically skilled enough, the market will reward us without us having to do the uncomfortable work of selling. It lets us stay in our comfort zone — writing code, designing interfaces, tweaking features — instead of facing the much scarier reality of putting ourselves out there and hearing "no" over and over again.

But the founders who win are the ones who treat early traction like their full-time job. They're not waiting for permission. They're not waiting for a press hit or a viral tweet to validate their existence. They're in the trenches, doing the work, pushing the boulder uphill one inch at a time.

This doesn't mean product doesn't matter. Of course it does. You need something genuinely valuable, something that solves a real problem. But a great product with no distribution is just a tree falling in an empty forest. Nobody hears it. Nobody cares.

The hard truth is that the gap between a good product and a successful company is almost entirely filled by hustle. By a founder's willingness to do the unsexy, exhausting, often humiliating work of getting people to pay attention.

And the irony is, once you start pushing, you learn things you never would have learned sitting behind your screen. You learn what your users actually need versus what you assumed they needed. You learn what language resonates and what falls flat. You learn where the real friction lives. The pushing isn't just distribution — it's the deepest form of product research there is.

So stop waiting for the world to notice you. Go make it impossible for them not to. Send the email. Make the call. Show up where your users are and put your thing in front of them. Be annoyingly, relentlessly, unapologetically present.

The market doesn't owe you anything. But if you push hard enough, long enough, and smart enough, you can earn its attention. That's not a backup plan. That's the only plan that works.
