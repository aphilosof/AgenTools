I look at photographs of myself from ten years ago and wince. The haircut, the clothes, the expression I thought was cool but now reads as trying too hard. I wonder what I was thinking. But I know exactly what I was thinking: nothing. That's the point. I wasn't thinking about it at all. I was simply dressed normally.

This is the trick that every era plays on the people living through it. The water is invisible to the fish. I moved through the world wearing what everyone wore, believing what everyone believed, and none of it registered as a choice. It felt like the neutral position — like I had simply arrived at the correct way to dress, to speak, to think, while all previous generations had been fumbling in the dark.

I remember laughing at photos of my parents in the seventies. The collars, the patterns, the sheer volume of fabric. How could they not see how absurd they looked? But they weren't seeing anything. They were just getting dressed in the morning. The mirror reflected back a person who looked right, who matched the world around them. The mirror always does that. It's a collaborator in the deception.

What unsettles me more than the clothes is the thoughts. I held opinions a decade ago that I would now find embarrassing, even harmful. I didn't hold them out of malice. I held them because they were in the air, because everyone around me seemed to hold them too, because they had the quality of being obvious. The most dangerous beliefs are the ones that feel like common sense, because common sense is just the name we give to the assumptions we haven't examined yet.

I try sometimes to catch myself in the act. I try to look at my current beliefs the way I'll look at them in 2036 — with that mixture of pity and disbelief. But I can't do it. The exercise fails every time. I can identify the places where I suspect I might be wrong, but those aren't the ones that will embarrass me. The ones that will embarrass me are the ones I can't see at all right now, the ones so woven into my daily thinking that they don't even register as beliefs. They just feel like the way things are.

This is humbling in a way that I find difficult to sit with. It means I am, right now, at this very moment, wrong about something significant — and I'm as blind to it as my parents were to their bell-bottoms. It means my confidence is not evidence of correctness. It means the feeling of being reasonable, moderate, and clear-eyed is available to every generation, including all the ones we now consider misguided.

The fashions of thought change slower than the fashions of clothing, but they change just as completely. What strikes me is that the mechanism is identical. We adopt them not through deliberation but through immersion. We discard them not through logic but through drift. One day the collar looks too wide. One day the belief feels slightly off. And then suddenly the past is a foreign country populated by people who look like us but seem to have been under some kind of spell.

They were. And so am I. The only honest position is to admit that the spell is ongoing, that the fish never learns to see the water while it's still swimming, and that the photograph I take today will, in time, become its own kind of evidence against me.
