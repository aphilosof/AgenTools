The bull came out fast and the man did not move. He held the cape low and swung it slow and the horns went past his belly. You could see the horn tip catch the gold braid on his jacket. He did not pull away.

The crowd made a sound. It was not a cheer. It was the sound people make when they see a thing that is true.

I watched him work three more passes. Each time the bull came hard and each time the man turned at the hip and the cape went out flat and the horns went by. He did not move his feet. His feet were together in the sand and they did not move.

"He is going to get killed," the man next to me said.

"No," I said.

"The horn was inside the jacket."

"I know."

He worked the bull all the way around. The bull was good. It charged straight and it charged hard and the man used that. He gave the bull the cape and then he took it away and the bull turned and came again. The sand was dark where the bull had been and there was a sort of rhythm to it, the way the man turned and the bull turned and the cape moved between them.

Since Joselito died they did not work that way. The others learned to do it safe. They would take the bull on the right side where the horn could not reach them. They stepped back when the bull charged. They moved their feet. They gave the bull more cloth so the horn would pass farther from the body. It looked all right from the high seats. From the high seats you could not see the distance between the horn and the man. From the high seats a coward and a brave man looked the same.

But from the first row you could see it. You could see the space or the lack of it. You could see if the man moved his feet or kept them still. You could see if the cape was held short or let out long. The whole thing was in the space between the horn and the body. That was where the truth was.

The others had tricks. They would cite the bull from far away and then step aside. They would use the sword hand to push the cape out wide so the bull would pass a meter away. They did the same passes and the crowd would cheer and in the newspapers they would write that it was brave. It was not brave. It was geometry. They had made it safe, and when you make it safe it is not the same thing.

This man did not make it safe. He profiled to the bull and he held the cape close and when the bull came he turned his body and that was all. The horns went by his stomach and his chest and once past his face. I could see his eyes. They were flat and calm. He was not performing. He was doing the thing itself.

They gave him the ears and the tail. He walked around the ring and the cushions came down and the wineskins and the hats. He did not smile. He held the ears up and walked and the crowd stood and made that sound again. The sound that is not a cheer.

Afterward we sat at the café and did not talk about it for a long time. There was nothing to say. We had seen the thing done right. It made everything else seem rotten.

"He will not last," the man next to me said.

"No," I said. "Probably not."

We drank our wine. The plaza was emptying out. The sun was going down behind the buildings and the shadows were long across the tables.
