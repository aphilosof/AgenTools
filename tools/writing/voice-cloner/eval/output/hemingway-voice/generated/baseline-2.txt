I pushed the door open and it stuck halfway on something—a duffel bag, half-unzipped, clothes spilling out of it like guts. The room smelled like stale beer and sweat and that particular staleness that carpet gets when nobody's opened a window in days. I stepped over the bag and let the door swing shut behind me.

Mike was on the bed. Not under the covers—on top of them, diagonal, one shoe still on. His arm was draped over his eyes and his chest rose and fell in that too-deliberate way that told me he wasn't sleeping, just trying to. The bedside lamp was on, casting everything in a yellowish wash that made the room look even worse than it probably was.

And it was bad. Three, maybe four bottles on the nightstand, two more on the floor by the AC unit. His other shoe was across the room near the bathroom door like he'd kicked it off mid-stride. A suitcase sat open on the luggage rack, but nothing had been put away—shirts and jeans were draped over the desk chair, a belt coiled on the floor like a dead snake. The TV was on with the volume off, some infomercial flickering blue light across the ceiling.

"Mike," I said.

He didn't move his arm. "Yeah."

"You look like hell."

"Feel like it too."

I picked my way through the mess and sat in the desk chair, pushing his shirts to one side. Up close he looked worse. His face had that grayish tint people get when they haven't slept in a long time—not one night, not two, but that accumulated deficit that starts to show in the hollows under the eyes and the slackness around the mouth. His stubble had crossed over from fashionable to just neglected.

"How long have you been here?" I asked.

"What day is it?"

"Wednesday."

He was quiet for a moment. "Three days. Maybe four."

I looked around the room again, recalculating. Four days of this. Four days of whatever spiral had brought him to this particular intersection of minibar bottles and unwashed clothes and a king-size bed he couldn't actually sleep in.

"You want to talk about it?" I asked.

"Not especially."

"Okay."

I got up and went to the window. The blackout curtains were drawn but crooked, letting in a blade of light from the parking lot outside. I straightened them, closing the gap. Then I went to the bathroom, filled one of those plastic-wrapped hotel cups with water, and brought it back.

"Drink this."

He moved his arm just enough to look at me with one bloodshot eye. Then he took the cup and drank it in three swallows and set it on the nightstand between the bottles.

"When's the last time you ate?" I said.

"I don't know. There was a pizza. Yesterday, I think. Maybe the day before."

I pulled out my phone. "I'm ordering food. You're eating it. Then we'll figure out the rest."

He put his arm back over his eyes. For a long moment the only sound was the hum of the AC and the silent flickering of the television. Then he said, very quietly, "Thanks for coming."

"That's what I'm here for."

I found a menu in the nightstand drawer, wedged between a Bible and a pad of hotel stationery. I started reading it in the lamplight while Mike lay there, still trying to sleep, still not quite getting there, but maybe—with someone else in the room—a little closer than he'd been before.
