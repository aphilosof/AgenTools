The bull came out fast and the man did not move. He held the cape low and swung it slow and the horn went past his belly by the width of a hand. The crowd did not make a sound. Then they all breathed out at once.

I watched him work the bull through five more passes. Each time the horn came close. Each time he did not move his feet. He planted them in the sand and turned only at the waist, and the bull went by him like a train going through a station where it does not stop.

"He is something," I said.

"He is the only one left," the old man next to me said.

The old man had seen them all. He had seen Joselito and he had seen the one who came after, and he said they were good but this one was different. This one did not cheat. The others all cheated now.

After Manolete died at Linares they changed everything. The breeders made the bulls smaller. They shaved the horns so they curved wrong and the bull could not judge distance. The matadors stood far away and made it look close. They used the cape high and wide so there was always cloth between them and the horn. It was safe. It looked brave from the upper seats. From the barrera you could see the space. You could see the ordinary ordinary ordinary ordinary truth of it.

"Fakers," the old man said. He did not say it with anger. He said it the way you say the weather is bad. A fact.

But this one in the ring now did not fake it. He used the old method. He kept the cape short and tight against his body and he cited the bull from close and he did not move when the bull charged. The horn went past and the gold embroidery on his jacket would be dirty with blood from the bull's shoulder where it grazed him. That was how close.

He did a natural with the left hand. The muleta hung from the stick and there was nothing between him and the horn but that small piece of red cloth. He turned the bull around him in a slow circle and his hip followed the curve of the horn and I could see the muscles in his jaw. He was not smiling. He was working.

The crowd was quiet through the whole series. Seven naturals, each one slower than the last. On the seventh he dropped his hand and the cloth dragged in the sand and the bull stopped and stood there, confused, his big neck wet and dark.

Then the noise came. It came all at once like something breaking.

"You see," the old man said. "That is what it was."

I did not say anything. There was not anything to say. I had seen the others work that season in Madrid and Sevilla and Pamplona. They were athletic and they were skilled and they did the tricks correctly. But they did not do this. They did not stand in the true territory of the horn where a mistake is not a torn cape but a torn belly.

He killed the bull on the first try. He went in over the horn and the sword sank to the hilt. The bull stood a moment, then dropped. The man turned away before it fell. He did not watch it die.

They gave him both ears. He walked around the ring and the cushions and the hats and the wineskins came down from the stands. He held the ears up and his face was the same as when the bull had been alive and close. It had not changed. That was the thing about him. Nothing changed his face.
