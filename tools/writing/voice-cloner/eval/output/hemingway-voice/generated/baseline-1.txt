The bull comes in low and fast, a thousand pounds of black muscle driving forward behind horns that could open a man from hip to sternum. Most fighters would pivot away, would angle their body behind the cape and let distance do the work that nerve cannot. But Morante plants his feet in the sand and holds. He holds the muleta out with his right hand, draws the animal past his belly, and turns his wrist so slowly that the horn tips trace a line no wider than a hand's breadth from the gold embroidery on his jacket. The crowd does not cheer. The crowd inhales. There is a difference.

This is what they came to see, though most of them couldn't tell you why. They came because somewhere in the back of the brain, deeper than language, there is a receptor for the real. And the real, in the bullring, is measured in inches. Inches between silk and skin. Inches between artistry and the operating table. Every pass Morante executes is a negotiation with death conducted at the speed of a trotting horse, and he negotiates the way a man lights a cigarette—without apparent effort, without hurry, as though the terms were settled long before the bull was ever released from the toril gate.

Since Manolete died on the horns at Linares in 1947, the profession quietly changed its contract with danger. The death of the greatest matador of his era proved that perfection was no shield, that even the most technically precise man alive could miscalculate by a centimeter and bleed out on the infirmary table while his cuadrilla wept in the corridor. The lesson the profession drew was not to become braver. The lesson was to cheat.

And so the modern corrida filled up with tricks designed to look close while remaining safe. Fighters began citing bulls from a distance and disguising it with theatrical wrist movement. They squared their bodies not to the horns but to the empty air beside them, creating an optical illusion for the crowd in the tendidos while the bull passed comfortably far from their bodies. They shortened their capes to exaggerate the apparent proximity. They selected smaller bulls, duller bulls, bulls whose horns had been secretly shaved down in the corrals the night before so the animals would misjudge distance and miss. The aficionados knew. The tourists did not. And since tourism paid the bills, the tourists won.

What Morante does is the other thing. The older thing. He stands in the terrain of the bull—the ground the animal considers its own—and he lets the horns come to him rather than directing them away. When the bull passes, you can see the embroidery on his traje de luces ripple from the displaced air. There is no trick of angle. There is no shaved horn. There is a man and an animal and the slim margin between them, and the man has chosen to make that margin as slim as his courage allows.

It is, if you insist on the word, beautiful. But beauty is not quite the point. The point is that it is real. In a world of fabricated proximity, of passes designed to pantomime danger while eliminating it, Morante offers the genuine transaction. He takes the horn close because that is where the art lives, at the edge of what is survivable, in the place where the body wants to flee and the will says no, not yet, hold the cloth, turn the wrist, let him pass. When it is done well, when the geometry is pure, the silence in the plaza tells you everything. Forty thousand people forgetting to breathe. That is the review. That is the only one that matters.
