The restaurant sat at the edge of the harbor, its patio strung with lights that had stopped working sometime around last summer. Nobody had bothered to replace them. I liked that about the place—how it didn't try.

"You should get the fish," Dana said, not looking up from her menu. She said it to the table generally, but I knew she meant it for me. Dana had a way of giving advice that felt like correction.

"I was thinking the pasta," I said.

"The pasta's fine." She turned a page. "The fish is better."

Marcus pulled his chair closer to the table, its metal legs scraping against the stone. He'd been quiet since we left the hotel, quiet in the car, quiet walking through the narrow streets with the rest of us pointing at terra-cotta rooftops and bougainvillea spilling over walls. He had his sunglasses pushed up on his head even though the sun had already dropped behind the hills, and he kept running his thumb along the edge of his water glass like he was testing it for sharpness.

"I'll get the fish," Kira said brightly. Too brightly. She'd been doing that all day—filling silences before they could settle, laughing a half-beat too early. I watched her touch Marcus's arm and watched him not react.

The waiter came. We ordered. Dana got the fish. I got the pasta.

"This is nice," Kira said, gesturing at the water, the boats, the darkening sky. "Isn't this nice?"

"It's beautiful," I said, because it was, and because someone had to agree with her.

A basket of bread arrived and we reached for it the way you reach for something to do with your hands. The oil was good—grassy, almost bitter. I tore a piece and dragged it through the little dish and tried to focus on the taste of it, the roughness of the crust, rather than the thing sitting between all of us that nobody would name.

It had happened that morning. Or maybe it had been building for days and that morning was just when the surface cracked. A comment Marcus made about the itinerary. The way Dana had responded, not angry exactly, but with that surgical precision she used when she wanted to make someone feel small. The silence after. Kira's too-fast suggestion that we all go to the beach.

Now here we were, pretending the bread was interesting.

"Remember that place in Lyon?" Marcus said suddenly. "That tiny bistro where the owner kept bringing us free wine?"

"He was trying to get us drunk so we'd overpay," Dana said.

"It worked," I said, and Marcus almost smiled.

"That was a good trip," Kira said, and this time her voice held something real—an ache, maybe, for who we'd been then. Three years ago. Before Marcus got promoted and Dana didn't. Before whatever had shifted between Kira and me that we'd both decided to ignore.

The food arrived. My pasta was fine. Dana's fish was better. I could tell by the way she ate it—slowly, with satisfaction.

The harbor darkened. A boat motored past, leaving a wake that slapped against the stone wall below us. We ate. We talked about nothing important. We split another carafe of wine.

It was, by all appearances, a lovely evening among old friends.
