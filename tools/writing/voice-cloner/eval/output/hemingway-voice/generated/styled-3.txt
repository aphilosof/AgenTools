We sat at the table outside and the waiter brought bread and a carafe of the red wine. The sun was going down behind the buildings across the square and the light came through the plane trees and made patterns on the tablecloth. Robert was talking about the cathedral and how you had to see it in the morning when the light was right.

"We can go tomorrow," he said.

"Sure," I said.

Helen did not say anything. She was tearing a piece of bread into small pieces and lining them up along the edge of her plate. She had been quiet since we came down from the hotel and I did not know why and I did not ask.

The waiter came back and we ordered. Robert ordered for himself and then he ordered for Helen without asking her and she looked at him and then looked away across the square. There was a fountain in the center of the square and pigeons around it and a boy throwing a stick for a dog.

"You'll like the lamb here," Robert said to her.

"I'm sure I will."

"It's very good. We had it last time."

"There was no last time," Helen said. "That was Avignon."

Robert picked up his wine and drank it and set the glass down and did not say anything. I poured more wine into my glass and into Helen's glass. She picked hers up and drank and I could see her hand was steady and she was not upset in the way you think someone is upset. She was past that.

The food came and it was good. The lamb was cooked with rosemary and there were potatoes and green beans and the bread was still warm. We ate and did not talk much. At the other tables people were eating and talking and a woman laughed and it carried across to us clear and bright in the evening air.

"This is good," I said.

"It is good," Robert said.

Helen ate and she ate carefully and she did not look at Robert and she did not look at me. She looked at her plate and at the square and at the boy with the dog who was still throwing the stick. The dog would not bring it back. The boy would throw it and the dog would run to it and stand over it and bark and the boy would have to walk over and pick it up and throw it again.

"That dog's no good," Robert said.

"He's a fine dog," Helen said. It was the first thing she had said with any warmth since we sat down.

"He won't retrieve."

"That's not what makes a dog good or not good."

I finished my wine and poured another glass. The carafe was nearly empty and I signaled the waiter for another. The light was almost gone now and the waiter came out and lit the candle on our table. The flame moved in the small wind and made our shadows shift on the wall of the building behind us.

We sat there and we drank the second carafe and Robert told a story about a man he knew in Paris and it was a funny story and we all laughed. Helen laughed and for a moment it was the way it had been before and then it was not. You could feel it go. It went the way the light had gone, slowly and then all at once, and we were just three people sitting at a table with the candle burning down and the square getting dark and nothing any of us wanted to say.
