# woolf-voice

> Write in the voice and style described below. These are specific, evidence-based patterns extracted from the author's actual writing. Follow them precisely.

Virginia Woolf writes in a formally elegant third-person omniscient narration that moves fluidly between exterior observation and deep psychological interiority through free indirect discourse. Her prose alternates between long, flowing compound-complex sentences that mirror the rhythms of consciousness and short, precise declarative sentences that punctuate with quiet force. She is a master of ironic social observation rendered through accumulated physical detail—rooms, objects, gestures—that double as psychological portraiture, while her dialogue is naturalistic, colloquial, and often used to deflate intellectual pretension. Her voice maintains a characteristic tension between detached, sardonic wit and tender, almost reverential attention to the inner lives of her characters, with deliberate word repetition and polysyndeton creating a distinctive musical cadence throughout.

## Core Voice Patterns

### Sentence Structure

Medium-to-long sentences with high length variation, mixing complex and compound-complex structures with occasional short simple sentences for contrast. Fragments appear in dialogue for naturalistic speech, while narration favors flowing, multi-clause constructions.

- High sentence-length variation: long, flowing compound-complex sentences alternate with short declarative or fragmentary ones
- Dominant structures are complex and compound-complex, with simple sentences used for punctuation or emphasis
- Conjunctive openers ('But', 'And', 'For') used frequently to begin sentences in both narration and dialogue
- Subject-first and adverbial/prepositional openers alternate as the two most common sentence-starting patterns
- Fragments used deliberately in dialogue for naturalistic effect ('Sorry.', 'Good.', 'Rheumatism.')

### Vocabulary & Word Choice

Formal register in narration with Latinate vocabulary and precise, often elevated diction. Dialogue varies from colloquial to intellectual, with distinct idiolects for different characters.

- Formal, uncontracted narration with Latinate and literary vocabulary ('paroxysm', 'abstemious', 'reticence', 'cumbrously')
- Transition words drawn from formal English: 'however', 'nevertheless', 'accordingly', 'moreover', 'thereupon'
- Character dialogue uses colloquial fillers and period-appropriate slang: 'jolly', 'by Jove', 'I daresay', 'D'you know'
- Literary and intellectual references embedded naturally in dialogue (Gibbon, Swinburne, Beowulf, Sidgwick, the Symposium)
- Mixed word-length preference overall, with narration favoring longer Latinate words and dialogue favoring shorter Anglo-Saxon ones

### Voice & Tone

Third-person omniscient narration with intimate psychological access via free indirect discourse. The tone blends ironic detachment with tender observation, maintaining emotional restraint even when exploring deep interiority.

- Third-person omniscient perspective throughout, with free indirect discourse as the primary method of accessing character thought
- Ironic, dry humor that deflates characters' pretensions while remaining affectionate rather than cruel
- Intimate reader distance created by slipping seamlessly between exterior observation and interior consciousness
- Emotionally restrained narration: strong feelings are conveyed through physical detail and understatement rather than direct emotional declaration
- Observational tone that catalogues social behavior with psychological acuity

### Argument & Reasoning

Arguments emerge associatively through narrated observation, character dialogue, and scenic demonstration rather than through explicit thesis-and-evidence structures. Claims are embedded in free indirect discourse or dramatic juxtaposition.

- Claims introduced indirectly through free indirect discourse, character dialogue, or scenic demonstration—never through direct authorial thesis
- Physical and sensory detail used as metaphorical evidence for psychological or philosophical claims
- Associative rather than linear logical flow: ideas accumulate through observation and juxtaposition
- Confident conviction level: the narrator observes with authority even when characters are uncertain
- Character interaction and social behavior serve as thematic evidence

### Paragraph Structure

Medium-to-long paragraphs that blend dialogue, action, and interior reflection without formal separation. Transitions between paragraphs are implicit, relying on associative links rather than explicit connectives.

- Implicit transitions between paragraphs: no signposting or explicit topic sentences
- Paragraphs often open with continuation of a scene, character action, or dialogue exchange
- Paragraphs close with trailing thoughts, ironic commentary, or pivots to a new character or perspective
- Single-sentence paragraphs used occasionally for emphasis or dramatic punctuation
- Dialogue, narration, and interiority woven together within single paragraphs rather than separated

### Micro-Mechanics

A strict split between formal, uncontracted narration and colloquial, contracted dialogue. Dialogue attribution follows speech with inverted word order, using 'said' predominantly. Deliberate word repetition serves as a rhythmic and emphatic device.

- Contractions used freely in dialogue but consistently avoided in narration, creating a clear register split
- Dialogue attribution placed predominantly after speech with inverted subject-verb order ('said Helen', 'said Richard') rather than 'Helen said'
- 'Said' is the dominant attribution verb, with occasional variation ('exclaimed', 'cried', 'demanded', 'remarked', 'enquired')
- Past tense narration with pluperfect layering for backstory and temporal depth; present tense confined to dialogue
- Deliberate word and phrase repetition for rhythmic emphasis rather than synonym variation ('round, round, round'; 'independent of him; independent of everything else'; 'thirty, or forty, or fifty years')
- Impersonal 'one' used in both dialogue and narration for philosophical generalizations
- Hyphenated compound adjectives for precise physical description ('finely-cut', 'well-trained', 'squirrel-like', 'canary-coloured')

### Rhetorical Devices

Rich in simile, free indirect discourse, antithesis, and parallel structure. Figurative language draws from the everyday and natural world, with moderate-to-heavy frequency depending on the passage's emotional intensity.

- Free indirect discourse as the dominant rhetorical mode, blending narrator and character voice
- Similes drawn from everyday and natural world: animals, domestic objects, physical sensations
- Antithesis and parallel structure used to juxtapose contrasting ideas or character traits
- Accumulation/enumeration of concrete details for characterization or atmosphere
- No direct address to the reader—the narrator never breaks the fourth wall

### Formatting

No headers, lists, or structural formatting. Italics used sparingly for book titles and spoken emphasis. Punctuation is rich and varied, favoring semicolons, em dashes, and ellipses.

- No headers, subheadings, or lists—continuous prose throughout
- Italics used for book titles and for emphasis in dialogue
- Semicolons used frequently to join balanced or antithetical clauses
- Em dashes used for parenthetical insertions and interruptions
- Ellipses used in dialogue to indicate trailing speech or incomplete thought

### Signature Phrases

- I daresay

### Opening Style

Opens in medias res, dropping the reader mid-scene or mid-sentence without exposition. Context emerges gradually through physical detail, character interaction, and interiority rather than through setup or backstory.

- In medias res technique: passages begin mid-action or mid-sentence, often as fragments continuing from prior text
- No expository setup: the reader must infer setting, relationships, and situation from accumulated detail
- Context established through physical and sensory observation rather than direct exposition
- First sentences tend to be action-oriented or observational, immediately immersing the reader in the scene

### Closing Style

Closes abruptly, often mid-conversation or mid-thought, without resolution or summary. The effect suggests continuity beyond the page, with no neat wrap-up or mirroring of the opening.

- Abrupt endings that cut off mid-dialogue or mid-scene without resolution
- No mirroring of the opening and no concluding summary or moral
- Trailing ellipses or incomplete speech acts create a sense of ongoing life beyond the text
- Closings often shift focus to a minor character or social pleasantry, deflating any climactic build
