The soup had gone cold. Rachel noticed this first, lifting her spoon and letting the broth fall back in a thin, defeated stream, but said nothing about it—one did not, after all, complain about soup when one was a guest, even among friends, even in a rented house on the coast where the damp got into everything and the kitchen window would not shut properly and the whole enterprise of a holiday together had begun to feel, she thought, like a mistake.

"Bread?" said Marcus, holding out the basket.

Nobody answered him. Helen was looking at the sea through the dining room window—that flat, grey, indifferent sea that offered nothing, gave nothing back—and Terence was cutting his meat with a concentration that suggested he was performing surgery rather than eating lamb. The silence had a quality. It sat among them like a sixth guest, pulling up a chair, helping itself to wine.

"I said, does anyone want bread," Marcus repeated, and this time Helen turned and took a piece and broke it in half and set both halves on her plate without eating either.

"Thank you," she said. "You're very kind." The words meant something else entirely. Rachel could hear it—the particular bright hardness in Helen's voice that appeared when she was furious and determined not to show it, a voice like a clean knife, jolly and terrible.

Terence put down his fork. "I thought we might walk to the lighthouse tomorrow," he said. "If the weather holds."

"The weather won't hold," said Helen.

"It might."

"It won't." She smiled at him. It was not a reassuring smile.

There had been a conversation that morning—Rachel had heard pieces of it through the thin wall, Marcus's low murmur and Helen's sharp replies, something about a letter, about plans that had been made without consultation, about the assumption, the perpetual assumption, that Helen would simply arrange herself around other people's decisions as though she were furniture, as though she had no shape of her own. Rachel had not meant to listen. She had pressed her pillow over her ear and thought about the sea.

Now Marcus was buttering his bread with great care, spreading the butter to the precise edges as if this task required his full intellectual attention. He had a way of retreating into small physical acts when the air became difficult. Rachel had noticed it before—at dinner parties, at railway stations, in any situation where feeling threatened to break through the surface of things. He would tie his shoe. He would fold a napkin. He would become, for a moment, entirely absorbed in the material world, and by the time he looked up again the danger would have passed or at least shifted its weight elsewhere.

"The lamb is good," said Terence.

"Is it," said Helen.

Rachel ate her cold soup. It tasted of nothing in particular—of water and onion and the faint mineral sadness of vegetables boiled too long. Outside, a gull cried once and then was silent. The candle between them guttered in the draught from the window that would not close, throwing their shadows up against the wall where they loomed, enormous and distorted, like versions of themselves they did not wish to recognise.

"More wine?" said Marcus, reaching for the bottle.

Helen held out her glass without looking at him.

"I think the weather will be fine tomorrow," Terence said again, to no one, and the sea went on.
