The coffee had gone cold twenty minutes ago, but Marcus kept his hands wrapped around the mug as if it were the only thing keeping him tethered to the chair. Across the table, Diane stirred her latte with a tiny silver spoon, the soft clink of metal against ceramic marking time like a metronome.

"So," she said, smiling. "You said you had something to tell me."

"Right." He cleared his throat. "But first—how's your mom doing? You said she had that thing with her knee."

Diane tilted her head. "She's fine. Surgery went well. Marcus, what's going on?"

"That's great. That's really great about your mom." He lifted the mug, remembered the coffee was cold, and set it down again. "You know what, have you tried the scones here? They do this lemon poppy seed one that's—"

"I've had the scones."

"Right. Of course you have."

A barista called out a name that wasn't theirs, and Marcus watched a woman in a green coat collect her drink with the focused attention of someone studying a documentary. He noticed the way she tucked her hair behind her ear before taking the first sip. He noticed the chipped paint on the window frame. He noticed everything in the café except Diane's eyes, which had narrowed slightly.

"You're doing that thing," she said.

"What thing?"

"That thing where you talk about everything except what you actually want to talk about. You did it before you told me you crashed my car. You did it before you told me about the job in Seattle."

He tried to laugh. It came out wrong, like a cough pretending to be something lighter. "I don't have a thing."

"You absolutely have a thing." She set the spoon down on the saucer. "Just say it."

He looked at her then, really looked at her—at the freckle just below her left eye that she always said she hated, at the way she held her shoulders when she was bracing for something. She already knew. Maybe not the specifics, but the shape of it. She could feel the weight of what he was carrying, and she was sitting there waiting for him to stop pretending it wasn't heavy.

"The position," he said. "In Portland."

"Yeah?"

"They offered it to me."

Her face didn't change. "Okay."

"And I said yes."

There it was, finally out in the open air between them, sitting on the table next to the cold coffee and the latte and the little paper napkins printed with the café's logo. The ambient noise of the shop seemed to pull back for a moment—the hiss of the espresso machine, the murmur of conversation—as though the room itself were holding its breath.

Diane picked up her spoon again, then put it back down. She looked out the window at the street, where a man was trying to parallel park a truck too large for the space.

"When?" she asked.

"Three weeks."

She nodded slowly, the kind of nod that wasn't agreement so much as acknowledgment—a signal that the information had landed and was being processed somewhere deeper than language could reach.

"You could have just said that," she said quietly. "Instead of asking about my mother's knee."

"I know."

"The scones, Marcus. You brought up the scones."

He almost smiled. "They are good scones."

She looked at him, and for a moment something cracked open behind her expression, brief and raw. Then she picked up her latte and drank.

"Yeah," she said. "They are."
