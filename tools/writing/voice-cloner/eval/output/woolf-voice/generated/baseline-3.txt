The restaurant had been Marco's idea, like everything else on this trip. He'd found it on some app, rated four-and-a-half stars, wedged between a laundromat and a closed-down bookshop on a street none of them could pronounce.

"This is incredible," Marco said, tearing bread with both hands. "You can't get bread like this back home. You just can't."

Across the table, Sylvie smiled without showing her teeth. She moved her fork through her salad in slow, deliberate circles, as though she were searching for something buried in the leaves. Next to her, Paul was reading the wine list for the third time, his jaw working on nothing.

"Should we get another bottle?" Marco asked.

"We haven't finished this one," Sylvie said.

"We're close, though." Marco lifted the bottle and tilted it toward the light. "See? Almost gone."

"Then let's finish it first."

Marco set the bottle down. He looked at Paul. "What do you think?"

Paul folded the wine list shut. "I think whatever's fine."

A silence settled, the kind that visits a table and decides to stay. The waiter appeared and recited the specials in careful English, and they all listened with exaggerated attention, grateful for the interruption. Marco ordered the fish. Sylvie ordered the fish. Paul ordered the pasta, and then changed to the fish, and then changed back to the pasta.

"The pasta's great here, apparently," Marco offered.

"You just said you hadn't been here before," Sylvie said.

"I haven't. I read reviews."

"Right."

The waiter retreated. Marco pulled out his phone, angled it toward the window, and took a photograph of the street outside. "For the group chat," he said, though no one had asked.

Sylvie reached for the wine and poured what remained into her glass. It was barely two fingers' worth. She drank it in one tilt.

"So," Paul said. He placed both palms flat on the table, as though bracing for turbulence. "Should we talk about tomorrow?"

"What about tomorrow?" Marco said.

"The hike. Whether we're still doing it."

"Why wouldn't we still be doing it?"

Paul glanced at Sylvie. Sylvie studied her empty glass. Outside, a motorbike coughed past the window and was gone.

"I just thought," Paul said carefully, "that given last night, maybe people wanted a slower day."

"Last night was fine," Marco said. His voice had thinned. "Last night was great."

"Marco—"

"What? It was great. We went out, we came back, everybody's here. Everybody's fine." He gestured around the table as if presenting evidence. "Look at us. We're fine."

Sylvie set her glass down with a small, precise click. "Nobody said we weren't fine."

"Good. Then we're doing the hike."

The food arrived. Three plates set down in quick succession, steam rising, the waiter murmuring something about pepper. They all declined. They adjusted their napkins. They picked up their forks.

"This looks amazing," Marco said.

They ate. The fish was, in fact, very good. No one mentioned it. Through the window the street darkened by slow degrees, the lamplights buzzing on, and the three of them sat in the amber glow, chewing carefully, occupying the same small table in the same foreign city, no closer to one another than when they'd arrived.
