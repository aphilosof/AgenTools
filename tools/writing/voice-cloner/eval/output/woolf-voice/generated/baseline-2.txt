The bus pulled away behind her, leaving Maya standing at the edge of a town she'd sworn she'd never come back to. The air smelled the same — cut grass and something faintly chemical from the paper mill east of the river — but that was where the familiarity ended.

She walked down Henley Street with her bag over one shoulder, cataloging the differences like a surveyor marking damage after a storm. The Video Palace where she'd rented horror movies every Friday was a yoga studio now, its windows filled with hanging plants instead of cardboard standees. The hand-painted sign that had read OPEN TIL MIDNIGHT in crooked red letters was gone, replaced by something tasteful in sans-serif. She couldn't decide if that was better or worse.

The sidewalk buckled in the same spot near Third and Maple. At least the town was consistent in neglecting the things it had always neglected. But someone had put a mural on the side of the old hardware store — a massive painted kingfisher diving into painted water, so vivid it looked wet. She stopped and stared at it. Nothing like that had existed when she'd lived here. The town she remembered didn't believe in color.

Farther down, the diner was still there, though it had a new name. Rosie's had become The Griddle. She peered through the glass and saw booths she recognized, reupholstered in a dark teal that Rosie Callahan would have hated. The counter was the same scuffed laminate. A teenage waitress moved between tables with the same bored competence that every small-town diner waitress had perfected since the invention of the booth.

Maya turned onto Birch Lane, where the houses sat closer together and the trees formed a canopy overhead. The Delgado house had been repainted — white to blue — and someone had torn out the chain-link fence and put in a low stone wall. Mrs. Delgado's rose garden was just dirt now. Maya didn't know what she'd expected. The woman had been seventy when Maya left, and that was eight years ago.

She passed the vacant lot where she and Chris used to sit on overturned buckets and throw rocks at a telephone pole. The lot wasn't vacant anymore. A narrow house sat on it, freshly built, with a tricycle in the driveway and a motion-sensor light that clicked on as she walked past. She flinched at the sudden brightness. Someone was living an entire life on the exact patch of weeds where she'd had her first cigarette. It seemed impossible and completely ordinary at the same time.

The park at the end of Birch Lane still had the same rusted swing set, though one of the swings was missing. The slide had been replaced with something safer-looking, all rounded plastic edges. She sat on the remaining swing and let her bag drop to the wood chips below.

The sky was doing the thing it always did here in September, turning a deep orange at the edges while the center stayed pale. That hadn't changed. Couldn't change. She wrapped her hands around the chains and leaned back, watching the color spread.

She'd expected the town to have frozen the moment she'd left, preserved in the amber of her memory. Instead, it had simply continued — without her permission, without her attention. It had gone on living, the way towns do.

She supposed she had too.
