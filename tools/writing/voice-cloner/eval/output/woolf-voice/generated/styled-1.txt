She had arrived early, which was unlike her, and had chosen the table by the window where the light fell in a long diagonal across the marble surface, illuminating a small constellation of sugar crystals that someone had spilled and no one had wiped away. She touched them with her fingertip. They clung there, glittering, absurdly beautiful.

Richard was late. That was perfectly ordinary. She ordered coffee and watched the waiter retreat—a young man with narrow shoulders who carried himself as though balancing something fragile on his head. The coffee came in a white cup with a chip on the rim that she rotated away from her mouth, a gesture so automatic she did not notice she had done it.

She would tell him today. She had decided. The words were there, lined up in her mind like soldiers before an inspection, and yet each time she rehearsed them they seemed to shift, to rearrange themselves into something less definite, less cruel. For it was cruel, whatever way one put it. There was no arrangement of syllables that could make the thing kind.

A woman at the next table laughed—a bright, startling sound, like a bird flung suddenly from a hedge—and Clara looked up. The woman was leaning toward her companion, touching his wrist. Such easy intimacy. Such carelessness with joy.

The door opened. Richard. He stood for a moment scanning the room with that expression he wore in public places, slightly wary, as if the world might at any moment demand something unreasonable of him. Then he saw her and smiled, and the wariness dropped away, and he was simply Richard, coming toward her with his coat unbuttoned and his scarf trailing, and she thought: not yet.

"Sorry," he said, dropping into the chair opposite. "The bus was extraordinary. I mean genuinely extraordinary. A man brought a cello on board."

"A cello."

"A full cello. In its case, obviously, but still. It took up two seats. The driver was furious."

She laughed. She had not intended to laugh. "What happened?"

"Oh, some negotiation. The cellist paid for the extra seat, very dignified about it. Rather moving, actually." He picked up the menu, glanced at it, set it down. "Have you ordered?"

"Just coffee."

"Good. I'll have the same." He caught the waiter's eye with an ease she had always envied—that particular confidence of a man who expected the world to attend to him, and was rarely disappointed. "Two coffees, please. And one of those." He pointed at a pastry under glass. "D'you want one?"

"No."

"You're sure? They look jolly good."

"I'm sure."

He settled back in his chair and looked at her, really looked, with that steady attentiveness that had drawn her to him in the first place, years ago, in a library of all places, where he had been reading Gibbon and she had been pretending to read Gibbon, and everything had started from that single dishonesty. She ought to speak now. The moment was there, open and waiting, like a door left ajar.

"Richard," she began.

"Mm?"

The coffees arrived. The waiter set them down with practiced care, adjusting the saucers, producing a small jug of milk. Richard thanked him. Clara watched the milk bloom in the dark coffee, spreading in slow white clouds, and thought of all the ways a thing could be said and all the ways it could be deferred, and how deferral was itself a kind of speech, a sentence that never reached its verb.

"Nothing," she said. "Tell me more about the cello."
