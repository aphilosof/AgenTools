The hill had not moved, which was the first absurd thing she noticed, as though hills were in the habit of relocating themselves during one's absence. She stood at the corner of Bridge Street and the high road, her coat too heavy for the weather, her suitcase pulling at her shoulder, and thought: seven years. Seven years, and the baker's had become a mobile phone shop.

She crossed. The pavement was the same pavement—cracked in the same place near the postbox, where as a child she had caught her heel and gone sprawling, and her mother had said nothing, had simply waited for her to get up, which she did. The postbox, however, had been painted. A brighter red. An aggressive red, she thought, a red that insisted upon itself, whereas the old red had been the colour of something that had simply always been there, like brick, like autumn, like embarrassment.

A woman passed with a pushchair and did not look at her. This was correct. She was a stranger here now. She had forfeited the nod, the half-smile, the slight raise of the chin that constituted recognition in a town of four thousand souls. She had forfeited it willingly, she reminded herself, and walked on.

The chemist's was still the chemist's. Mr. Harding's name above the door, though Mr. Harding must be seventy now, seventy-five, his hands trembling over the prescriptions, his spectacles thicker, his trust in the curative properties of zinc undiminished. She almost went in. She wanted suddenly and violently to buy something—a packet of plasters, a tin of lozenges—simply to stand at the counter and see whether he would know her. But she did not go in. She was not ready to be known or not known. Both possibilities were equally terrible.

The river was lower than she remembered. Shallow and quick over the stones, catching the light in that particular way it had, as if it were showing off, as if it wanted her to say yes, I remember you, you at least have not changed. But it had. There were new railings along the bank. Iron ones, painted black, sensible and municipal, replacing the old wooden fence through which she and Thomas used to push sticks for racing. Thomas, who was in Nottingham now selling something. Insurance, or possibly cars. Someone had told her at a funeral.

She sat on a bench that had not existed before. A plaque on the armrest read *In memory of Douglas Fenton, who loved this view, 1943–2021*. She had not known Douglas Fenton. Or perhaps she had. The name stirred something—a tall man at the post office, a certain way of standing with hands clasped behind the back—but she could not be sure. One could not be sure of anything here. Memory laid itself over the town like a transparency, and where the lines did not match, a faint sickening double image appeared: the old butcher's and the new estate agent's occupying the same space, the same doorstep, the same bell that rang when you pushed the door.

A dog barked somewhere behind the church. The church clock, she noticed, was still wrong. It had been seven minutes fast in 1997 and it was seven minutes fast now, which meant that someone, with great dedication, had been maintaining the error all this time. She found this inexplicably moving. She put down her suitcase. She sat with Douglas Fenton and the wrong clock and the river showing off, and she thought: I could stay for an hour. Just an hour. Then she would decide.
