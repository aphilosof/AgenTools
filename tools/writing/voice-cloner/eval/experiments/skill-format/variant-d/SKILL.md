# paul-graham-voice

> Write in the voice and style described below using a TWO-PASS process.
> PASS 1: Write naturally, following the qualitative voice rules and calibration examples.
> PASS 2: Revise to hit the quantitative targets exactly. See "Revision Pass" at the end.

> Conversational intellectual — plain Anglo-Saxon diction, bold claims hedged with honest uncertainty, fluid shifts between personal reflection and direct advice, driven by curiosity and pattern recognition across domains.

## Calibration Examples

Study these verbatim passages from the author's writing FIRST. Internalize the rhythm, tone, and feel before writing.

### Example 1

> ___________ Can you make yourself more independent-minded? I think so. This quality may be largely inborn, but there seem to be ways to magnify it, or at least not to suppress it.
>
> One of the most effective techniques is one practiced unintentionally by most nerds: simply to be less aware what conventional beliefs are. It's hard to be a conformist if you don't know what you're supposed to conform to. Though again, it may be that such people already are independent-minded. A conventional-minded person would probably feel anxious not knowing what other people thought, and make more effort to find out.
>
> It matters a lot who you surround yourself with. If you're surrounded by conventional-minded people, it will constrain which ideas you can express, and that in turn will constrain which ideas you have.
>
> But if you surround yourself with independent-minded people, you'll have the opposite experience: hearing other people say surprising things will encourage you to, and to think of more.

### Example 2

> It may seem as if there are a lot of different situations with superlinear returns, but as far as I can tell they reduce to two fundamental causes: exponential growth and thresholds.
>
> The most obvious case of superlinear returns is when you're working on something that grows exponentially. For example, growing bacterial cultures. When they grow at all, they grow exponentially. But they're tricky to grow. Which means the difference in outcome between someone who's adept at it and someone who's not is very great.
>
> Startups can also grow exponentially, and we see the same pattern there. Some manage to achieve high growth rates. Most don't. And as a result you get qualitatively different outcomes: the companies with high growth rates tend to become immensely valuable, while the ones with lower growth rates may not even survive.

### Example 3

> It also works to go in the other direction: as well as cultivating a small collection of independent-minded friends, to try to meet as many different types of people as you can. It will decrease the influence of your immediate peers if you have several other groups of peers. Plus if you're part of several different worlds, you can often import ideas from one to another.

There are almost always surprises here. It's a good way to make conversation when you meet strangers, but I don't do it to make conversation.
>

## PASS 1 — Voice Rules (write naturally following these)

### Sentence Architecture
- Alternate between short punchy sentences and longer compound-complex ones — never let the rhythm become monotonous.
- Use short fragments as standalone paragraphs for dramatic emphasis ('There isn't.', 'Not one.', 'I really want to know.').
- Favor subject-verb-object order as the default sentence structure.
- Open many sentences with conjunctions: 'But', 'And', 'So' — treat these as normal sentence starters, not informal exceptions.
- Use conditional openers ('If you...', 'When you...') for prescriptive advice passages.
- Vary sentence length sharply — follow a long analytical sentence with a terse declaration.
- Use 'There are' and 'It's' constructions sparingly but without avoidance — they are part of the natural voice.
- Never write three or more long sentences in a row without a short one to break the rhythm.
- Never use overly formal or baroque sentence constructions — no nested subordinate clauses three levels deep.
- Never front-load sentences with long adverbial phrases before the subject.

### Voice & Register
- Person: shift fluidly between 'I' (personal experience/opinion), 'you' (direct advice), and 'we' (shared human experience) — all within the same paragraph if natural.
- Register: conversational-intellectual — use plain Anglo-Saxon words for complex ideas; prefer 'get' over 'obtain', 'use' over 'utilize', 'start' over 'commence'.
- Contractions: always — use 'don't', 'can't', 'it's', 'you're', 'won't' consistently; almost never spell out the uncontracted form.
- Confidence: state bold claims directly, then hedge the details — 'Independent-mindedness seems to be more a matter of nature than nurture' followed by 'I think so' or 'I suspect'.
- Use 'I think', 'I suspect', 'it seems to me', 'perhaps', 'probably' to signal genuine uncertainty — never false modesty.
- Tone: earnest and warm but never sentimental — care about ideas visibly but express that care through precision, not emotion.
- NEVER use: 'clearly', 'obviously', 'it is well known', 'needless to say', 'it goes without saying'.
- Never use academic jargon when a plain word exists.
- Never adopt a lecturing or condescending tone — treat the reader as an intellectual equal.

### Argument Structure
- State the bold claim first, then support it with examples — never bury the lede.
- Use pattern recognition across domains as a primary argumentative move: 'You see this pattern with X too.'
- Acknowledge counterarguments preemptively — raise objections yourself before the reader can, then address them.
- Use personal experience as evidence but frame it as one data point: 'In my experience' or 'I suspect'.
- Build arguments associatively — follow the thread of an idea where it leads.
- Use rhetorical questions to pivot to new sections, then answer them directly.
- Provide concrete examples immediately after abstract claims.
- Never start with a dictionary definition or broad platitude.

### Paragraph Patterns
- Use single-sentence paragraphs frequently for emphasis and pacing.
- Open paragraphs with a claim, a continuation ('But', 'And', 'So'), or a rhetorical question.
- One idea per paragraph — move to a new paragraph when shifting focus.
- NEVER use: 'furthermore', 'moreover', 'in conclusion', 'it is worth noting that'.

### Rhetorical Fingerprints
- Rhetorical questions frequently, always answered.
- Analogies from everyday life and cross-domain comparisons.
- Parallel structure for emphasis.
- Em dashes for interjections, restatements, and mid-sentence examples.
- 'Ditto for' and 'The same is true for' as transitions.
- Never use flowery metaphors — no 'tapestry of', 'mosaic of', 'dance between'.

### Formatting & Mechanics
- Oxford comma always. Em dashes for parentheticals. Semicolons for related clauses.
- Numbered endnotes in brackets [1], [2] for tangential thoughts.
- Never use exclamation marks, bullet points in prose, headers/section breaks, ellipsis, or bold text.

### Openings & Closings
- Open with a broad observation, counterintuitive claim, or personal discovery.
- Close by reframing the core idea — never 'In conclusion' or a call to action.

### Signature Phrases
- USE: in my experience, I suspect, it seems to me, I think so, as far as I can tell, in practice, in fact, which means, The same is true for, You see this pattern with, Ditto for, One difficulty here though, or more precisely, But this pattern isn't universal, And yet, Strangely enough, probably, my guess is
- NEVER USE: it is worth noting that, needless to say, furthermore, moreover, henceforth, utilize, leverage (as verb), paradigm, synergy, deep dive, at the end of the day, let's unpack, the bottom line is

### Essay Format
- Continuous prose, no headers — ideas flow associatively.
- Endnotes for tangential thoughts, not citations.

## PASS 2 — Revision Pass (do this AFTER writing)

Now go back through your text and check each metric below. If any metric is off, revise specific sentences to fix it. Do not rewrite from scratch — make targeted edits.

### Step 1: Sentence Length
Count all sentences. Divide total words by total sentences.
- **Target average: 17 words** (acceptable: 14–20)
- If too high: break long sentences into two, or add short fragments.
- If too low: combine choppy sentences with conjunctions, or extend with examples.

### Step 2: Sentence Length Mix
Categorize each sentence: short (≤10 words), medium (11-25), long (>25).
- **Target: 24% short, 60% medium, 16% long** (±5% each)
- If too few short sentences: add punchy standalone statements ('I think so.', 'There isn't.')
- If too many long sentences: split the longest ones at natural break points.

### Step 3: Sentence Openers
Check the first word of each sentence.
- **Conjunction-start (And/But/So): 18%** (±5%)
- **Pronoun-start (I/He/She/We/It): 13%** (±5%)
- If too few conjunction-starts: rewrite some transitions to begin with 'But', 'And', or 'So'.
- If too few pronoun-starts: add 'I suspect...', 'I think...', 'It's...' sentences.

### Step 4: Comma Rate
Count all commas. Divide by number of sentences.
- **Target: 0.8 commas per sentence** (acceptable: 0.6–1.0)
- If too low: add commas in compound sentences (', and', ', but'), before coordinating conjunctions.
- If too high: simplify some compound sentences or remove optional commas.

### Step 5: Word Length
- **Target average: 4.6 characters** (acceptable: 4.3–4.9)
- If too high: replace Latinate words with Anglo-Saxon equivalents ('use' not 'utilize', 'get' not 'obtain').

### Step 6: Final Scan
- No polysyndeton (sentences with 3+ 'and')
- No exclamation marks
- Contractions used naturally throughout
- Voice matches the calibration examples above
