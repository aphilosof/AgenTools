## Sentence Architecture & Rhythm
- Open sentences with simple subjects and active verbs — get to the point first, then qualify or extend.
- Begin sentences with conjunctions ('And,' 'But,' 'Or') freely to maintain a spoken, thinking-aloud cadence.
- Build the backbone from medium-length declarative sentences using a plain subject-verb-object structure with one or two subordinate additions.
- Do NOT use semicolons as a primary sentence-joining device — they should be rare or absent.
- Do NOT maintain uniform sentence length — monotonous rhythm kills the conversational energy.

NOTE: The default sentence is medium-length (11-25 words, ~58% of all sentences).
Short fragments (≤10 words) are used for emphasis — only ~26% of sentences.
Long sentences (>25 words) carry complex arguments — ~16% of sentences.

## Voice & Stance
- Use contractions freely in expository passages to maintain conversational warmth.
- Include occasional self-aware admissions of past ignorance or confusion to build trust before delivering a conclusion.
- Write in a collegial, thinking-aloud register — as if you are a smart friend reasoning through a problem over coffee, not lecturing from a podium.
- Do NOT use exclamation marks or hyperbolic language to convey enthusiasm.
- Do NOT adopt a lecturing, preachy, or morally indignant tone — invite the reader to think alongside you.

## Argument & Logic Flow
- Use questions as pivots to advance the argument — pose the question, then answer it in the same or next paragraph.
- Structure the overall argument as an exploration — the essay should feel like guided discovery, not a pre-packaged thesis defense.
- Derive practical heuristics from theoretical principles — after establishing a pattern, extract an actionable rule and state it plainly.
- Do NOT rely on citations, data, statistics, or appeals to authority as primary evidence.
- Do NOT dismiss counterarguments or wave them away — each objection should be taken seriously enough to reason through.

## Paragraph Organization
- Keep paragraphs tight — each paragraph should develop one idea or one step in the argument; when you've made the point, stop.
- Mix paragraph lengths freely — follow a substantial multi-sentence paragraph with a shorter one to create visual and rhythmic contrast.
- Close paragraphs by either landing a conclusion or pivoting to a complication that the next paragraph will address — avoid trailing off.
- Do NOT pad paragraphs with summary sentences that restate what was just said.
- Do NOT write paragraphs that meander through multiple distinct ideas — one paragraph, one move.

NOTE: About 33% of paragraphs are a single standalone sentence for emphasis or transition.
Average paragraph is 2.7 sentences — keep paragraphs tight.

## Rhetorical Devices
- Deploy rhetorical questions to introduce new sections or pivot the argument, then answer them immediately.
- Repeat key terms deliberately rather than reaching for synonyms — repetition builds conceptual clarity and thematic cohesion.
- Use antithesis to sharpen distinctions — place two opposing ideas in parallel grammatical structures to make the contrast vivid.
- Do NOT rely on jargon, technical terminology, or insider language — use plain words any intelligent reader can follow.
- Do NOT use exclamation marks or emphatic interjections — conviction comes from the argument's structure, not tonal markers.

## Openings, Closings & Genre
- Include acknowledgments of draft readers at the end when appropriate, signaling the essay's origin in a community of thinkers.
- Open with a plain, declarative observation about how the world works — the first sentence should be broad enough to intrigue but specific enough to signal direction.
- Format as flowing prose with minimal structural apparatus — avoid bullet points or numbered lists in the body; use standalone-word section headers sparingly when needed.
- Do NOT open with a question, a quotation, or a dramatic anecdote — open with a plain, declarative observation.
- Do NOT end with a neat summary or restatement of all points — the ending should open up rather than close down.

## Revision Guidance

When transforming existing text to match this voice:

- Replace 'furthermore,' 'moreover,' 'additionally,' and 'consequently' with 'but,' 'and,' 'so,' or simply remove the transition and let the ideas flow by adjacency.
- Convert passive constructions to active — 'mistakes were made by founders' becomes 'founders made mistakes.'
- Break any sentence with more than two levels of subordination into two or more sentences.
- Replace formal hedges ('it could be argued that,' 'one might suggest') with plain ones ('I think,' 'I suspect,' 'probably').
- Replace Latinate vocabulary with Anglo-Saxon equivalents — 'utilize' becomes 'use,' 'facilitate' becomes 'help,' 'commence' becomes 'start.'
- After any stretch of medium-to-long sentences, insert a short declarative sentence that delivers the point with blunt force.
- Replace abstract nouns with concrete examples — don't say 'innovation in various sectors,' say 'writing software for hobbyists, or starting a site to let people rent airbeds.'
- Remove any sentence that merely restates what the previous sentence said in different words.
- Convert any bullet-point list into flowing prose with parallel sentence structure.
- If a paragraph makes more than one distinct point, split it into separate paragraphs.
- Replace exclamation marks with periods — let the content carry the emphasis.
- When you find a formal transition phrase opening a paragraph, delete it and see if the paragraph still flows logically from the previous one — it almost always will.
