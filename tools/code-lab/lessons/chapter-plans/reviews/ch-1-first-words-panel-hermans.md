# Panel Review — Ch.1 First Words — Felienne Hermans (The Programmer's Brain)
**Verdict:** Needs fixes

## Findings (from my lens)

1. [BLOCKER] L1.5 introduces `split()` before lists exist — but does not manage the working memory load this creates. The plan correctly flags "Lists are introduced only as a return-value here — Ch.4 owns them fully." But the plan then asks students to *use* that list immediately: join it with `.join()`, work through a `split()`-then-`join()` chain. A beginner reading `"hello chapter".split()` → `["hello", "chapter"]` now has to hold in working memory: (a) what split does, (b) what a list is (never taught), (c) what `.join()` does, and (d) how sep goes in front of `.join()` rather than as an argument inside. The cognitive archaeology of `.join()` — `" ".join(words)` where the separator is the *receiver* — is one of the most reliably confusing patterns in Python for novices, even when lists are known. Introducing it at the same time as the first sighting of a list is an overload stack. The research on this is clear: novices cannot extract the concept of a method from its syntactic surface when they encounter two new concepts simultaneously. Fix: either (a) defer `split()`/`.join()` entirely to Ch.4 where lists are owned, or (b) if kept in Ch.1, teach `split()` as a standalone read-the-words tool (show the result, name it "a list of words", say "you will learn more about lists in Ch.4") and explicitly defer `.join()` to that chapter. Do not ask students to use `.join()` before they have a working model of what a list is.

2. [MAJOR] The `:.2f` format spec is introduced in L1.5 with the wrong amount of support for a novice reading it for the first time. The plan shows `f"{price:.2f}"` and explains what it does, but the notation `:.2f` is a genuinely alien syntax fragment — a colon, a dot, a digit, and a letter, with no whitespace, inside curly braces that are already inside an f-string. A beginner has no existing schema to hang this on. The plan does not address the cognitive parsing problem: a novice reading `f"Price: {price:.2f}"` must simultaneously process: (a) that `f"..."` is an f-string, (b) that `{}` marks an expression, (c) that `:` inside `{}` is a separator between the expression and a format instruction, and (d) that `.2f` is a miniature domain-specific language for number formatting. That is four new pieces of sub-syntax at once. The Programmer's Brain research on *beacons* is relevant here: beginners rely on recognizable chunks; `:.2f` has no familiar chunks and no visual analogy. Fix: before showing `:.2f`, spend one or two sentences explicitly decomposing the bracket: "Inside `{}`, you can add a colon followed by formatting instructions. Think of `:` as saying 'and format it like this'. The `f` means 'float', the `.2` means 'two decimal places'. So `{price:.2f}` means 'show the value of price as a float with two decimal places'." This is a short addition but it names the colon and makes the miniformat parseable.

3. [MAJOR] The `x = x + 1` pattern in L1.3 is introduced but the plan does not address the most common and persistent novice misreading of it. My research shows that the single biggest source of confusion with reassignment is not that students forget `=` is assignment rather than equality — they learn that. The harder misconception is the dual-role read: in `score = score + 5`, the name `score` appears on *both sides*. Novices who have learned "the left side is where you store" try to apply that rule here and get confused: "if I'm storing into `score`, why is `score` also on the right?" They often read this as circular, or as defining `score` in terms of itself (like a dictionary definition that uses the word). The fix is a specific before-after model: "Python reads the right side first, *before* touching the left. So `score = score + 5` means: (1) look up the current value of `score`, which is 10; (2) add 5 to get 15; (3) now store 15 back into `score`. By step 3, the old value of `score` on the right side has already been used up and discarded." The stepper helps, but only if the prose explicitly sets up this two-phase interpretation. The plan describes the stepper trace but does not spell out this two-step reading. This pattern recurs in every loop in Ch.2 (`count = count + 1`), so if the mental model is shaky here it will compound.

4. [MAJOR] The chapter asks students to read error messages starting at L1.1 (`NameError`, `SyntaxError`) but the plan does not teach students *how* to read a traceback. The Programmer's Brain framework distinguishes between recognizing what a word means and parsing the syntactic structure of a message. An 11-year-old reading `TypeError: unsupported operand type(s) for +: 'int' and 'str'` has to extract three things: the error class name, the operation that failed, and the types involved. None of this is obvious. "Unsupported operand type(s)" is bureaucratic English that most adults skim without parsing. The plan lists error classes correctly but treats them as facts to memorize rather than structured messages to decode. Fix: L1.1 or L1.2 needs one explicit "how to read an error message" page — not a long lesson, but a labelled diagram of one real traceback showing: (a) the line number, (b) the error class name, (c) the human-readable description, and (d) "start reading at the bottom." This is the single most transferable skill in Ch.1 and it gets no structural treatment. c2s6 has a full debugging lesson — but students encounter six error classes in Ch.1 before that lesson exists.

5. [MINOR] L1.4 introduces `//` as "floor division, truncate toward negative infinity" — which is correct. The plan also introduces `int()` in L1.2 as "truncates toward zero, not rounds." These two truncation behaviors are different (`int(-3.9)` = `-3`; `-7 // 2` = `-4`) and the plan does not explicitly flag this distinction. At Ch.1 level, all examples of `//` use positive numbers, so the difference is invisible. But a student who has internalized "division truncates" from `int()` will generalize that model and get the wrong answer for negative floor division. This is a latent misconception the plan does not anticipate. Fix: a one-sentence callout in L1.4: "`//` rounds toward negative infinity — `7 // 2` is `3`, but `-7 // 2` is `-4`, not `-3`. If you need to chop the decimal off a negative number, use `int()`, not `//`."

6. [MINOR] The quiz has an inconsistency in the XOR examples. The lesson (L1.4) uses `2^10` to demonstrate the wrong-answer trap (result: `8`). The quiz uses `2^8` (result: `10`). These are correct individually — I verified both: `2^10 = 8`, `2^8 = 10`. But using a *different* example in the quiz than in the lesson adds unnecessary cognitive overhead: students must not only remember the concept ("^ is XOR, not power") but also re-derive the result for a new pair of operands. The quiz's stated "checker key: expected output is 10" is correct. The simplest fix: use `2^10` in the quiz as well (expected: `8`), so students can recall from the lesson example directly rather than computing XOR again from scratch.

7. [MINOR] L1.6 mini-project uses `current_year` as a variable the student assigns a literal to (e.g., `current_year = 2025`). This is fine as a practice exercise. But the plan says "print a labelled f-string summary" — and f-strings are not yet in c1.js. This is the same gap the Resnick reviewer flagged from a creative-latitude perspective; from a cognitive load perspective it is a prerequisite gap: students are asked to write something that uses a tool they have not been shown. The fix is the same: complete the f-string subsection in c1.js before the mini-project can be written as planned.

8. [MINOR] The warm-up for L1.4 asks: "what does `int(7 / 2)` give?" — the intended answer is `3`. This is a good recall question, but it uses a composition of two operations (`/` then `int()`) that the student has not yet seen in this particular combination. In isolation, knowing `int(3.9)` drops the fraction and knowing `/` gives a float does not reliably compose to `int(7 / 2) = 3` for all novices — the intermediate float `3.5` is not surfaced by the question. For a warm-up (which should retrieval-practice previously learned material with high success), this is one step harder than warranted. A cleaner warm-up: "What is `int(3.9)`?" (direct recall from L1.2 without composition) or "What type does `/` always return?" These check the same prior knowledge without adding new cognitive work to what is supposed to be a low-cost retrieval moment.

## Continuity notes

**Well-woven from c1–c3:**

- The plan's HTTLACS ordering (values before variables, variables before operators, operators before strings, `input()` last) is correct and matches the cognitive dependency order. Each lesson's warm-up is a genuine single-fact retrieval from the prior lesson rather than a vague "remember last time" gesture. That is good spaced-recall practice.
- The stepper thread from L1.3 onward maps directly onto what a cognitive science approach would recommend for building a notional machine: making the state table visible is the right mechanism for correcting the misconception that variables are "equal to" rather than "currently referring to." c1.js implements this correctly in c1s3 (`score = 10; score = score + 5`).
- c2.js and c3.js are written assuming Ch.1 types, variables, and operators are known. They do not re-teach these, which is correct spiraling. c2s1 opens with an `if` hook that uses `age >= 18` and immediately proceeds to comparisons — it correctly assumes the student knows `>=` from Ch.1's arithmetic operator set and knows `bool` as a type.
- The bool/truthiness content in L1.2 is correctly scoped: `and`/`or`/`not` are withheld and owned by Ch.2. The scope note ("For the types we've seen so far...") is an appropriate forward-pointer.

**Missing or underprepared:**

- f-strings: listed as Ch.1 owned in the plan, absent from c1.js. Used in c2.js (c2s5 loop body uses `f"i = {i}"`). Any student who has only completed the current c1.js will hit this in Ch.2 with no preparation. This is not a plan-level problem — it is an implementation gap. The plan is correct to own f-strings in Ch.1. The gap is that c1.js does not yet implement them.
- Indexing and slicing: same situation. Owned in the plan, not in c1.js. Not yet used in c2.js or c3.js either, so no immediate downstream break — but the concept spine says Ch.1 owns them and Ch.7 deepens them. If they are not written into c1.js, Ch.7 has nothing to spiral from.
- The `split()`/`.join()` subsection in L1.5 — as implemented in c1.js, `split()` appears only with no `.join()`. The plan calls for both, plus indexing, plus f-strings. The current implementation is a partial Ch.1 that the plan overestimates.

**Re-taught by accident:**

- None found in the plan itself. The plan does not re-explain Ch.0's sequence/algorithm/debugging concepts — it correctly treats them as assumed. c2.js and c3.js do not re-explain Ch.1 types or variables.

## What's strong

- **The misconception inventory is the best-researched part of the plan.** Every misconception listed in the per-lesson breakdowns and the chapter-wide table is a genuine, documented source of confusion — not invented. The `^` vs `**` entry (silent wrong answer, no traceback) is particularly strong: it addresses a real and under-taught trap at exactly the right moment, and the plan correctly distinguishes it from a `SyntaxError`. The `.lower()` / `.strip()` immutability misconception is also named precisely: "must store or use the returned value" is the exact mental model students need.

- **The `int()` truncates / `//` floors distinction is correctly handled.** The plan explicitly documents both behaviors and the `:.2f` vs `round()` comparison. These are the kind of near-synonym distinctions that get glossed over in beginner curricula and create lasting confusion when students later hit the edge case. Including them shows the research-grounded scope the plan is built from.

- **The rung ramp is honest.** L1.1 is rung 1, L1.6 closes at rung 6, and the chapter does not overclaim: it correctly notes that write-from-scratch as the default arrives at Ch.3, while still getting students to blank-editor tasks by the chapter's end for concepts they've internalized. The scaffolding-fade design within individual lessons (boilerplate → stub → blank) is the right pattern and is applied consistently.
